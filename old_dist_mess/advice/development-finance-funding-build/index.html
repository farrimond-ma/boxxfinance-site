Development Finance 101: Funding Your Next Build

Property development requires capital before value is realised.

Whether constructing new units, converting existing buildings or undertaking significant refurbishment, developers must fund land acquisition, build costs and professional fees before any sale proceeds are received.

Development finance is designed specifically for this purpose. When structured correctly, it allows developers to progress projects without overexposing working capital or relying on unsuitable long term borrowing.

Understanding how it works is essential before committing to a scheme.

What Is Development Finance?

Development finance is a short to medium term funding facility designed to cover the costs of property construction or major refurbishment.

Unlike traditional commercial mortgage funding, which is based largely on current property value and rental income, development finance is assessed on projected end value and build feasibility.

It typically funds:

  • Land acquisition
  • Construction costs
  • Professional fees
  • Contingency allowances

The lender releases funds in stages as construction progresses.

How Development Finance Is Structured

Development finance facilities are usually structured around:

  • Loan to cost ratio
  • Loan to gross development value
  • Staged drawdowns
  • Retained or serviced interest
  • Defined exit strategy

The projected end value of the completed scheme is central to lender assessment.

Clear cost schedules and realistic timelines significantly improve approval prospects.

The Importance of Exit Strategy

Development finance is not long term funding.

It requires a defined exit, typically through:

  • Sale of completed units
  • Refinance onto longer term investment finance
  • Transfer onto structured commercial mortgage funding

Lenders place significant weight on the viability of the exit plan.

Where a short term acquisition is required before development begins, bridging finance may be used to secure the site prior to arranging full development funding.

Planning this sequence carefully reduces risk.

What Lenders Assess

Development finance lenders typically review:

  • Developer experience
  • Project feasibility
  • Build cost breakdown
  • Planning status
  • Contingency allowances
  • Exit viability

Experience matters, but first-time developers can secure funding where professional teams and project fundamentals are strong.

Transparent costings and realistic projections are critical.

Managing Cash Flow During Build

Development projects often require careful liquidity management.

Because funds are released in stages, developers must plan initial outlays carefully.

In some cases, complementary facilities such as structured working capital funding may support associated business operations during the build period.

This ensures core trading activity is not restricted while development progresses.

Risk Considerations

Development carries inherent risk including:

  • Build cost overruns
  • Planning delays
  • Market shifts
  • Sales slowdowns

Prudent developers include contingency allowances and realistic timelines in their financial modelling.

Lenders will expect to see these considerations addressed clearly.

When Development Finance Makes Sense

Development finance is most appropriate when:

  • The project has defined planning status
  • Costs are clearly quantified
  • Exit route is realistic
  • Developer experience aligns with project scale

It is less suitable for speculative land acquisition without defined planning prospects. Each scheme must be assessed individually.

Final Thoughts

Development finance provides structured capital that enables projects to move forward before value is realised.

When planned carefully and supported by realistic financial modelling, it can facilitate profitable schemes while managing exposure.

The key lies in structuring the facility correctly from the outset and aligning it with a credible exit strategy.
